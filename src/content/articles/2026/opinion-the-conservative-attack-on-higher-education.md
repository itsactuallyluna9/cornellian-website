School board battles demanding Creationism be taught alongside Evolution. Fights over sex ed and visual-aid bananas. The culture war is nothing new in the U.S., but like so much else in 2025, attacks on higher education went from surgical strikes to a full-on revolution. In case you were focused on the ICE raids, the Epstein files, and the government shutdown—or if, like me, you also missed the storm brewing in higher ed long before this—here are some stories about attacks on academic freedom that you need to know.

## The National Wrecking Ball
President Trump has purged some government agencies and put unqualified loyalists in charge of others, but he has selected the Department of Education (DOE) for total demolition. This is a cornerstone goal of Project 2025[^1]. Officially titled Mandate for Leadership: The Conservative Promise, Project 2025 is a 900-page policy manual, published by The Heritage Foundation and other conservative organizations, designed to reshape the federal government by consolidating power in the executive branch and implementing right-wing policies.

In addition to calling for a litmus test on financial aid (“Taxpayers should expect their investments in higher education to generate economic productivity”), Project 2025 explicitly calls for the elimination of the DOE, which it claims is necessary to liberate higher education from woke “diversicrats”[^2].

Moreover, Project 2025 seeks to give states greater control over colleges: “As Washington begins to downsize its intervention in education, existing funding should be sent to states as grants over which they have full control, enabling states to put federal funding toward any lawful education purpose under state law”[^2].

This might sound innocuous. But by removing the federal watchdog that defends equitable access to education and the content of that education, what conservatives really want is for each state to have free rein on what is taught in its schools. Trump has said as much[^3]. In red states, conservatives have shown again and again that they cannot be trusted with this power, as they continue to attack academic freedom–the foundation of the liberal arts.

## State-Level Censorship
Here in Iowa, Governor Kim Reynolds and her Republican allies have been attacking academic freedom from every angle for years.

In late 2024, Iowa Speaker Pat Grassley (grandson of U.S. Senator Chuck Grassley) created the House Higher Education Committee[^4]. House Republicans appointed Rep. Taylor Collins to lead the newly formed committee. Though only a first-term representative at the time, Collins had already made a name for himself, The Gazette noted, by placing himself on the front lines of “the House GOP’s work to eliminatediversity, equity and inclusion programs at Iowa’s public universities and other conservative higher education reforms”[^4]. Speaker Grassley was clear about the new committee’s purpose at the time:
> We know one of the biggest issues facing our state is the workforce crisis. Iowa House Republicans have been proactive and innovative in our solutions to address this, including our efforts to refocus Iowa’s higher education system on producing students ready to fill high-need jobs in our state.[^4]

In a recent interview with KCRG News, Collins made no secret that he considers his mission to be reforming Iowa’s higher education system from top to bottom, and that this includes doing away with areas of study he considers too liberal[^5]. He has stated explicitly that he wants to eliminate all diversity, equity, and inclusion programs from Iowa campuses. The Gazette quoted Collins as saying, “My constituents are no longer interested in paying for garbage like the bachelor of science in social justice or gender studies…”[^6].

Collins tells the Gazette that colleges don’t “belong to the faculty”[^7] and that he intends to restrict what professors teach. This is a stunning attack on academic freedom that implies the politicians in Des Moines, not someone with a PhD, should have the power to decide what is taught in the classroom.

In an interview with KCRG, Collins vowed to introduce 20 bills this year to reform higher education[^8]. One of those bills includes requiring what Collins calls a “Charlie Kirk Lecture and Debate Series” at Iowa colleges[^5].


## New Mandates Reshaping Iowa Higher Education
Gov. Reynolds’ Board of Regents—which governs the University of Iowa, University of Northern Iowa, and Iowa State University (UI, UNI, ISU)—has echoed Collins’ sentiment. On August 13, 2025, the Board passed a policy stating that faculty may teach controversial subjects but must present a “range of scholarly views” and avoid “indoctrination”[^9]. The policy effectively allows the Board to discipline professors if they teach topics like structural racism without also validating “opposing” viewpoints. Critics, including Regent Nancy Dunkel—the lone dissenting voice left on the Board—argues this is an attack on academic freedom:

> Academic freedom is a principle that scholars, teachers, and students...have the freedom to teach, to learn, to conduct research, and publish or express ideas without fear of censorship, retaliation, or institutional interference, as long as those thoughts align with standards of scholarly integrity and professional ethics.[^10]

She further noted the absurdity of the new mandate:

> …[I]f a professor has to present both sides to an issue, does that mean a marketing professor must also include anti-capitalist arguments to students? Do anti-evolution arguments have to be presented in biology classes?[^10]

## Forklifts Over Philosophy
In October, Gov. Reynolds presented the recommendations of her Iowa Department of Government Efficiency Committee (Iowa DOGE). Led by corporate titans like Fareway CEO Reynolds Cramer (no relation to Board President Robert Cramer), Iowa DOGE recommended slashing millions from student aid for higher education[^11]. While this might arithmetically “save” money, does cutting funding for lower-income Iowans sound like a path to improving education in Iowa?

Iowa DOGE also recommends that Iowa taxpayers reimburse corporations like Fareway for the expense employers incur training their employees. The boldness of the hypocrisy is breathtaking. A wealthy grocery store CEO wants Iowa taxpayers to pay him to train his employees to run forklifts—a skill the report specifically cites[^12]--while simultaneously recommending slashing funding for actual academic degrees for lower-income students.

As if this weren’t bad enough, the Iowa DOGE report also calls for cutting financial aid for degrees that aren’t deemed “marketable” or “workforce ready”[^13], placing fields like music, art, and sociology on the chopping block. This creates a two-tiered system of citizenship. If you want to learn a trade or skill that benefits a corporation’s bottom line, the state will pay for it. If you want to study disciplines that you feel personally drawn to but don’t meet conservatives’ “high demand” litmus test, you’re on your own.

Perhaps Iowa DOGE’s most shameful recommendation is to completely remove the needbased requirement for financial aid. Just as the state removed income limits for private school vouchers, Iowa DOGE recommends that the children of millionaires be eligible for taxpayer-funded financial aid for college[^14]. Meanwhile, a lower-income student desiring a degree arbitrarily deemed not in high demand at the moment may be told the state can’t afford to help them pay for college.

This obsession with “marketable’ degrees fundamentally misunderstands—or perhaps intentionally ignores—the purpose of a liberal arts education. Historically, the goal has been not merely to train a worker for a single vocation, but to cultivate a versatile intellect capable of critical thought. By exposing students to a broad range of disciplines—from sociology to the natural sciences—a liberal arts education develops the complex problem-solving skills that allow graduates to connect disparate ideas, navigate diverse cultural contexts with empathy, and pivot successfully between careers, ultimately preparing graduates not just for employment, but for engaged citizenship and a meaningful life.

But perhaps that is exactly the problem. A liberal arts education also creates citizens who question authority and analyze systems of power. The DOGE recommendations suggest the state doesn’t want critical thinkers but compliant workers. They are willing to pay to train you how to operate a forklift, but they won’t pay for you to learn how to question why the man owning the forklift pays you so little.

## The Dark Money Blueprint
One thing is certain: what’s happening here in Iowa is nothing new. It is the culmination of a decades-long strategy pioneered by the Koch Brothers and their organization, Americans for Prosperity (AFP). AFP spends millions to fund a massive, permanent lobbying arm here in Iowa[^15] [^12], one that has been exposed pushing the Kochs’ anti-union agenda despite widespread Iowan support for unions[^16].

AFP also spends hundreds of millions to reshape higher education, so much so that there’s now a nationwide student movement to “Un-Koch My Campus”[^17]. The American Association of University Professors has written extensively about the Kochs’ ever-broadening influence on higher education under the guise of philanthropy[^18]. Over decades, the Kochs have poured nearly half a billion dollars into over 550 colleges and universities[^19]—not as charity, but as investment. Through their donations, they secure agreements that guarantee them influence over the hiring of faculty and the curriculum itself at the colleges they fund, effectively buying seats at the academic governance table in order to mold those colleges according to their right-wing vision for education[^18].

Their goal is to transform higher education from the laboratory of democracy into a factory that turns out unquestioning workers. They know they can’t win the debate in the classroom, so they buy the boardroom.
