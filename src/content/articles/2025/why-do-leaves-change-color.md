---
title: Why Do Leaves Change Color?
author: Tram Nguyen
pubDate: Nov 19, 2025
---
As Mount Vernon transitioned from the heat of summer to the chilly breeze of fall, Cornell has been engulfed by the multiple shades of red, orange, and yellow leaves. While some trees on the campus continued keeping their green shade, many have changed colors and left their branches. Why?

It starts at the pigments that the leaves could (and could not) produce. In order to get nutrients, trees go through photosynthesis—a process that takes sunlight, water and carbon dioxide to form glucose. However, when the leaves take in sunlight for photosynthesis, they produce chlorophyll. This is a pigment that makes leaves green. That’s why during spring and summer, where light is plentiful, leaves are green.

As seasons change, the leaves get less sunlight to work with. Due to the days getting shorter, there is less sunlight hitting the leaves. Thus, they produce less chlorophyll to make them green. As the green fades, it reveals colors that were hidden underneath the chlorophyll, such as xanthophylls and carotenoids. Essentially, xanthophylls are yellow pigments and carotenoids are orange pigments. These pigments make the leaves orange and yellow.

That is why leaves fall. Because of the lack of chlorophyll to make nutrients from sunlight, these leaves after revealing its underneath color dies out. Since the leaf no longer is able to produce as much nutrients, keeping them on the branches did nothing but withhold more energy from the trees to help those leaves survive. That’s why when leaves begin to lose chlorophyll (aka turn color) they quickly fall to the ground.

What about leaves that are red? Scientists are actually still debating the cause behind it. Because while red leaves seem to be a common part of fall, only 15% of the world’s trees produce red leaves. Indeed, red pigment is not there when the chlorophyll makes the leaves green like the yellow or orange pigment—which means that this red pigment appears out of nowhere! One theory could be that red leaves functioned as a form of protection from sunlight. With the lack of chlorophyll, anthocyanins (a fancy name for red pigment) protects the trees from the solar irradiation. Another theory could be that red leaves are an evolutionary trait in responding to insects. Scientists found that reddish trees often face less insects that require the use of pesticides or sprays by farmers.

While the origin of the red pigments still leaves room for interpretations, it is undeniable that these color variations painted our campus beautifully. As these leaves continue to fall, their trees will be prepped for the long, chilly season ahead.
